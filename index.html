On Randos

Nov 20th, 2014 9:44 pm

Encryption keys make possible many of the most convenient aspects of the internet today. Online banking, healthcare, and investing services rely heavily on passwords to ensure the safety of our data. “Secure” data transfer is a bit of misnomer: it is trivial for a malicious party to gain access to data transferring across the web. Thus the need for encryption, ensuring said data can only be read by the intended recipient. Random numbers are the backbone of generating encryption keys, but there’s one problem, humans, and computers, are TERRIBLE at generating passwords and random numbers, respectively. Computer programs follow pre-written instructions, and it is therefore impossible for them to generate truly random numbers. So what happens when we type rand into our Ruby programs? A pseudo-random number generator is used; in ruby’s case, a Mersenne Twister. By far the most common PRNG, the Mersenne Twister is unfortunately not robust enough for use in cryptography, as observing relatively few cycles enables the prediction of future output.

So what do? Enter the HARDWARE random number generator:

OneRNG provides a simple open source method for generating random numbers, relying on entropy from an avalanche diode circuit (your guess is as good as mine) and an RF receiver that channel hops among radio waves. Now, this does not provide true randomness, but is much better than Ruby’s Random.

You be asking, why mess with these imperfect solutions, surely there must be a way to generate a real random number for Pete’s sake! Take a look:

Radioactive particles decay stochastically, preventing an observer from predicting future decay. Be sure to use a Geiger counter if you go this route.

In order to prevent myself from relying on the .each method whenever an iteration is necessary, I’ve decided to take a closer look at methods that help reduce the need for multiple layers of nesting within my code. The .map (aka .collect) and .select methods drew my attention first. When called on an object, .map will iterate through the object, and return the evaluation of the accompanying code block into a new array.

The .select method allows us to evaluate a block of code for each element in the original array, and if the evaluation returns true, the method will populate a new array with the original value from the array upon which .select was called.

Finally I’d like to touch on an emerging technology in the web/tech sphere: mesh networks.

As this NPR article (http://www.npr.org/blogs/alltechconsidered/2014/09/29/352476454/how-hong-kong-protesters-are-connecting-without-cell-or-wi-fi-networks) documents, protestors in Hong Kong have faced a shutdown of their internet service accessed from their phones, leaving them unable to effectively organize themselves. Enter Firechat, a new app that’s been downloaded hundreds of thousands of times in Hong Kong over the last few weeks. Firechat enables phones to communicate directly with any other phones within wifi range. By linking these connections together into a mesh network, the protestors are able to communicate over the network, sans internet access. And, if at least one of the nodes in the network is connected to the internet, the other phones can access it by hopping from phone to phone until they reach the internet-connected node. The technology is still primitive: Firechat doesn’t even implement encryption, so anyone on the network can sniff the data their node relays. But it’s a start, and may well signal a future of the internet that does not have the functionality of “turning off”, as it does today with much more centralized access nodes.
